Benfits

Balance training helps reduce the risk of falls in older adults with balance problems and women with low bone mass. It also improves postural stability after a stroke. More research is needed to identify which components of balance training do the most good and to test them for preventing falls and injuries in healthy adults. So far, the evidence hasn't been sufficient for an official recommendation, such as the one for physical activity that most of us know (by heart!): To reduce your risk for chronic disease and preserve function, get at least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity activity (such as brisk walking) on all or most days of the week, plus resistance and flexibility exercise a couple of times a week.

Exercise 1 (Beginner): Sumo Squat With Outer Thigh Pulse

A great lower-body strength exercise that emphasizes the muscles of the inner thigh, as well as the glutes, quads, hamstrings, hip flexors, and calves.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Start in a wide stance, feet turned out 45 degrees.
  2. Bend at your knees and hips to lower into a sumo squat. Keep your torso upright.
  3. As you stand, extend one leg and the opposite arm. Hold and pulse your leg up 2 to 3 inches three times.
  4. Put your leg back down to start position and repeat.
  5. Alternate sides for 12 reps.

Exercise 2 (Beginner): Standing Crunch With Under-the-Leg Clap

Great for practicing balance and reducing wobble.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Start balancing on one leg, with the other out in front of you, knee bent at a 90-degree angle, and your hands together overhead.
  2. Crunch forward and clap your hands under the raised leg.
  3. Continue to clap over your head and under your raised leg 12 times. Repeat on your other leg.

Exercise 3 (Beginner): Curtsy Lunge With Oblique Crunch

Stronger legs and glutes; improved balance.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with your feet hip-width apart, fingertips at your ears and elbows out wide.
  2. Cross one leg behind you and lower into a curtsy lunge.
  3. Without rotating your hips, stand and bring that same leg up to meet the same side elbow for an oblique crunch.
  4. Do 12 reps, and then switch legs.

Exercise 4 (Moderate): Banded Triplanar Foot Taps

Helps improve core and improves balance.

Equiment Required

  • Resistance Band

Instructions

  1. Place a Versa-Loop band directly above the knees and shift into a single-leg, quarter-squat position.
  2. While balancing on the stance leg, tap the alternate foot forward, to the side and directly behind you.
  3. The core and hip muscles will fire to maintain single-leg balance against the band’s resistance in three different directions.

Exercise 7 (Intermediate): Single-Leg Deadlift With Side Bend

Promotes good synergy between hip hinge movement, core muscle, and shoulders.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Start by standing with feet together. Shift weight to left leg and lift right knee in front of body to hip height, knee bent to 90-degree angle.
  2. Extend arms to side at shoulder height.
  3. Hinging from hips and tightening core (imagine your navel squeezing back into your spine), extend right leg behind you to hip height as you reach right hand to touch inside of left ankle. (Don't forget to engage both left and right glute muscles)
  4. Release to start. Repeat eight times. Switch sides and repeat.

Exercise 8 (Intermediate): Curtsey Lunge With Oblique Crunch

Stronger legs and glutes; improved balance.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, fingertips at ears and elbows out wide.
  2. Cross right leg behind you and lower until back knee is about one to three inches off ground (into what's called a curtsy lunge).
  3. Rise and return right leg up to right side (careful not to rotate the hips), bend torso to right moving right elbow as close to right knee as you can (into a standing oblique crunch).
  4. Release to start and repeat 12 times. Switch sides and repeat.

Exercise 9 (Intermediate): High Plank Shoulder Taps

Build core muscle.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Get on floor on hands and knees, hands slightly wider than shoulders.
  2. Extend legs behind you until you’re balancing in one long line on hands and feet in high plank position. (To make it easier, bring knees down to floor like you would for modified push-up position)
  3. Bracing your core, lift right hand off ground and tap left shoulder. Slowly release hand to ground and switch sides, trying not to let weight shift as you do this. Do 10 reps each side.
  4. Struggling to maintain balance? Position feet wider.
  5. For more of a challenge, bring feet together or even do exercise with one foot off ground.

Exercise 10 (Challenge): Weighted BOSU Ball Squats

Excellent exercise that strengthens the quads, hamstrings, glutes and by using the bosu ball this helps to stabilise the core as well.

Equiment Required

  • Bosu Ball

Instructions

  1. Place a BOSU ball in front of you with the flat side up and the round side touching the floor.
  2. Add weight via dumbbells, weighted vest, barbell or EZ Curl Bar.
  3. Step onto the BOSU ball and adjust your footing until your feet are shoulder-width apart.
  4. Perform a standard Squat, lowering yourself to 90 degrees, keeping your back, knees and toes straight and your core tight.

Exercise 11 (Challenge): Single-Leg RDL With Press

Increases strength in the glutes, hamstrings and lower back, Improves hip stability, Improves balance.

Equiment Required

  • Weight or Dumbbell

Instructions

  1. Grab a dumbbell or kettlebell that is a challenging weight.
  2. Hold the weight in your left hand and stand on your right leg at the same time.
  3. Perform a Romanian Deadlift, making sure to keep your back, knee and toe straight and your core tight.
  4. At the top of the RDL, pause and perform a Single-Shoulder Press.
  5. Perform another RDL.
  6. Progress by performing the exercise on a BOSU ball as you get stronger.

Exercise 12 (Challenge): Leg-Elevated Bulgarian Split Squat

Strengthens the muscles of the legs, including the quads, hamstrings, glutes, and calves.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Place a bench or plyometric box behind you about knee-high.
  2. Add weight via dumbbells, weighted vest, barbell or EZ Curl Bar.
  3. Lift one leg up and place it on the bench or box behind you.
  4. Perform a Standard Split Squat, keeping your back, knee and toe straight and your core tight.

Strength training makes you stronger and fitter. This benefit is the obvious one, but it shouldn’t be overlooked. Muscle strength is crucial in making it easier to do the things you need to do on a day-to-day basis, especially as we get older and naturally start to lose muscle. Strength training also helps protect bone health and muscle mass. At around age 30 we start losing as much as 3 to 5 percent of lean muscle mass per year thanks to aging. Lastly, Strength training helps keep the weight off for good. Aerobic exercise such as walking, running, and cycling is well-known as a way to help increase the number of calories you burn in a day and thereby shed extra pounds. But strength training helps, too (even if you’re not burning a huge number of calories during the workout

Exercise 1 (Beginner): Squat

Strengthens and tones glutes.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with your feet slightly wider than hip-width apart, toes slightly turned out, arms at your sides, palms in.

Bend your elbows and bring your palms together in front of your chest. (You can also just hold your hands in front of your chest the entire time.)
  3. Drive through your heels to stand and squeeze your glutes at the top for 1 rep.

Exercise 2 (Beginner): Romanian Deadlift

Enhance movement patterning, and even help resist injury caused by lower back stress

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with your feet hip-width apart, knees slightly bent, arms relaxed by the front of your quads. This is the starting position.
  2. Hinge forward at your hips and bend your knees slightly as you push your butt way back. Keep your back flat and shoulders engaged as you slowly lower your arms along your shins toward the floor until you feel a stretch in your hamstrings.
  3. Keeping your core tight, push through your heels to stand up straight and return to the starting position. Keep your arms close to your shins as you pull. Pause at the top and squeeze your butt. That's 1 rep.

Exercise 3 (Beginner): Reverse Lunge

Strengthen glutes and core muscles.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with your feet together with your arms by your sides (or pictured) or hands on your hips. This is the starting position.
  2. Step back (about 2 feet) with your right foot, landing on the ball of your foot and keeping your heel off the floor.
  3. Bend both knees until your left quad and right shin are parallel to the floor, your torso leaning slightly forward so your back is flat. Your left knee should be above your left foot and your butt and core should be engaged.
  4. Push through the heel of your left foot to return to the starting position. This is 1 rep.
  5. You can either alternate legs each time, or do all your reps on one side before switching to the other side.

Exercise 5 (Moderate): Goblet Squat

Helps strengthen glutes.

Equiment Required

  • Dumbbell

Instructions

  1. To set up, grip a dumbbell vertically with both hands underneath the top of the weight.
  2. Position the dumbbell against your chest, and keep it in contact with your body throughout the movement.
  3. Stand with your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart and toes slightly out.
  4. Inhale and start to squat, sitting back in the hips, bending the knees and keeping the core tight.
  5. Allow the elbows to track in between the knees, stopping when your thighs are parallel to the ground.
  6. Push up through the heels back to the starting position.

Exercise 6 (Moderate): Woodchop

Helps strengthen core and arms.

Equiment Required

  • Dumbbell

Instructions

  1. Hold a dumbbell by each end to the right side of your body.
  2. Squat down slightly, rotating your trunk to the right.
  3. Begin to stand up and, keeping your arms extended, bring the dumbbell up and across your body by twisting your torso.
  4. Allow your right foot to pivot as you go. The dumbbell should end up over your left shoulder.
  5. Twist your torso back and return the dumbbell to the starting position.

Exercise 8 (Intermediate): Bent-Over Dumbell Row

Builds muscle for back and shoulders.

Equiment Required

  • Dumbbell

Instructions

  1. Stand next to a bench and lean forward at the waist, placing your inside hand and knee on the bench for support.
  2. Grasp a dumbbell in your outside hand with a neutral grip and fully extend your arm toward the floor.
  3. Keep your low back slightly arched, chin down and head neutral.
  4. Pull the weight toward your hip, making sure to keep your elbow close to your body throughout the movement.
  5. Squeeze your back hard at the top and slowly return to the start position.

Tones and strengthens glutes.

Instructions

Exercise 9 (Challenge): Squeeze Presses

Tremendous exercise for building functional strength and hypertrophy in the chest, triceps, and shoulders.

Equiment Required

  • Dumbbell

Instructions

  1. Lie on a bench, back flat against it, arms bent, and dumbbells held at chest height, palms facing away from your face.
  2. Actively press the ends of the dumbbells together.
  3. Continue squeezing the ends together as you press the dumbbells straight up over your chest.
  4. Then, lower the weights with control until your elbows dip just below the height of the bench
  5. Push them back up to start, continuing to squeeze the ends of the dumbbells together throughout the entire movement.

Exercise 12 (Challenge): Forward-to-Reverse Lunges

Work the glutes, legs and abs, each emphasizes different leg muscles.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand tall with your feet shoulder-width apart.
  2. Take one large step forward with one foot and lower your hips toward the ground with control.
  3. Return to standing by driving through the heel of your front foot.
  4. Once standing, take one large step behind you with the same foot.
  5. Sink into your hips as you lower toward the floor.
  6. Then, driving through the heel of your front foot, stand back up.
  7. Complete all repetitions on one side before switching legs.

Benfits

The idea of being flexible can easily be overlooked in terms of one’s physical health. Much like other aspects, flexibility has many health benefits. One such benefit is lowering the chance of injuring yourself during some of these other workouts. Developed flexibility allows the body to withstand more physical demand. With this, one’s body will also feel less sore and unlikely to experience muscle cramps. Another benefit is improved balance and posture. Improving flexibility extends the body’s range of motion and promotes a better posture. One’s overall strength and physical performance is also improved with better flexibility. Improved flexibility results in making muscles work more efficiently, leading in improved strength. A commonly forgotten benefit of better flexibility is an improved state of mind. Being able to stretch effectively promotes relaxation which can help with winding down. With these exercises, one could improve their flexibility and achieve these benefits.

Exercise 1 (Beginner): Shoulder Roles

This exercise is performed by rotating one’s shoulders in big circles. This is performed while standing and interchanging between clockwise and counterclockwise motions. Shoulder Roles promotes flexibility in the shoulder area and can also reduce tension in the neck.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with the feet hip-width apart.
  2. Let the arms hang down at the sides of the body.
  3. Breathe in and lift the shoulders up toward the ears.
  4. Move the shoulders back, squeezing the shoulder blades together.
  5. Exhale and drop the shoulders back.
  6. Move the elbows forward, feeling the stretch at the back of the shoulders.
  7. Repeat this 10 times.

Exercise 4 (Moderate): The Seated Butterfly

This exercise is beneficial for anyone at any level of exercise, including beginners. The Butterfly involves sitting on the floor and stretching one’s legs. This exercise targets the lower body. It helps relieve tightness in the hip region and promotes leg flexibility. It is recommended to start off your day with this exercise or to cap off a strenuous workout with it. It may be difficult to achieve proper form early on, but with repetition one’s form will improve over time.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Sit on the floor or a prop with the soles of your feet pressing into each other.
  2. To deepen the intensity, move your feet closer in toward your hips.
  3. Root down into your legs and sitting bones.
  4. Elongate and straighten your spine, tucking your chin in toward your chest.
  5. With each inhale, lengthen your spine and feel the line of energy extending out through the top of your head.
  6. With each exhale, fall heavy into the floor and relax or sink a bit more deeply into the stretch.
  7. Hold this position for up to 2 minutes.
  8. Repeat 2 to 4 times.

Exercise 10 (Challenge): Standing Forward Bend:

The Standing Forward Bend is an exercise where one stretches their upper body down to their lower body while standing. This technique is a more developed version of touching one’s toes for stretching. This stretches the hamstrings, calves and hips. It also strengthens one’s thighs and knees. This bend requires caution and slow progression to someone just starting out their flexibility exercises. With practice, it will prompt one’s flexibility greatly.

Equiment Required

  • None

Instructions

  1. Stand with your feet together.
  2. Place your hands next to your feet or on the ground in front of you.
  3. Inhale and extend your chest to lengthen your spine.
  4. Exhale and gently press both legs toward straight.
  5. On an exhalation, extend your torso down without rounding your back.

Benfits

Endurance is an important aspect for one’s physical health. The body’s overall endurance dictates how long it is able to withstand physical activity for a period of time. One can improve their endurance though specific exercises. Endurance training is generally related to aerobic exercises. These are exercises that require lots of moving and breathing, like running or swimming. There are many benefits with having good endurance. One benefit is having more energy for daily activities. With endurance training, one will feel less winded from physical activity. It also prompts good cardiovascular health. The breathing and increase in blood flow works wonders for the body. Endurance exercises are also some of the most effective ways to lose weight. These exercises increase one’s metabolism, which results in the body burning more calories. Endurance training is also good at preventing and controlling diabetes. These exercises help keep our glucose levels within a safe count. With all of these benefits, some of these simple exercises can do wonders for one’s body.

Exercise 1 (Beginner): Walking and Jogging

Walking or jogging are some of the most popular ways to exercise and increase endurance. Both walking and jogging are effective for improving endurance, the choice of which to do is really up to the individual. This activity can be achieved by going outside or utilizing a treadmill. While doing these activities, it is recommended to have good running shoes and to dress light/comfortably. These exercises are great for having multiple ways of being executed.

Equiment Required

  • Treadmill (Optional)
  • Running Shoes

Instructions

  1. Train three days a week.
  2. Jog or run/walk 20 to 30 minutes, two days a week.
  3. Take a longer run or run/walk (40 minutes to an hour) on the weekend.
  4. Rest or cross-train on your off days.
  5. Run at a conversational pace.
  6. Consider taking regular walk-breaks.

Exercise 10 (Challenge): Swimming

Swimming is a fun and popular way to improve one’s endurance. Swimming is one of the most effective exercises out there, since it exercises the entire body. It is easy on the body and is recommended to be done in 30 minutes on average. The exercises in the pool are quite simple as well, mainly being laps around the pool. Water sports, like water polo, are fun group alternatives as well. The challenging aspect of swimming is having access to a pool, which normally requires going to a gym.

Equiment Required

  • Pool
  • Swimwear

Instructions

  1. Begin swimming using the front crawl stroke.
  2. Turn at the end of the lane using an open turn, in order to not crash into the pool edge.
  3. Turn at the end of the lane using a flip turn to propel oneself into another lap.
  4. Repeat the process for 30 minutes, with 10 minutes for breaks.
